Chemistry Prize

About the prize

The Nobel Prize in Chemistry is an award given annually by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences to scientists in various fields of chemistry. It is one of five Nobel Prizes established by Alfred Nobel's will in 1895, to award outstanding contributions in chemistry, physics, literature, peace, and physiology or medicine. The prize is administered by the Nobel Foundation, and awarded by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences on the recommendation of the five-member Nobel Committee in Chemistry elected by the Academy. The awards take place annually in Stockholm on December 10, the anniversary of Nobel's death.

    Nationality: (born July 25, 1956) is an American

    Maria Skłodowska – Marie Curie

    Nationality: (November 7, 1867 – July 4, 1934) was a Polish physicist and chemist

    Award year: 1983

    Prize motivation: discovery of radium and polonium; isolation of radium

    She is considered the first woman in the study of radioactivity when she coined the term for it. Marie is also the first woman to receive a Nobel Prize, the first and only woman to have the honor of winning two Nobel Prizes in two different fields – physics and chemistry. She was also the first female university lecturer at the University of Paris (Sorbonne).

    Jacobus Henricus van 't Hoff

    Nationality: Netherlands

    Award year: 1901

    Prize motivation: laws of chemical dynamics and osmotic pressure

    His pioneering work helped found the modern theory of chemical affinity, chemical equilibrium, chemical kinetics, and chemical thermodynamics. In his 1874 pamphlet Van 't Hoff formulated the theory of the tetrahedral carbon atom and laid the foundations of stereochemistry.
